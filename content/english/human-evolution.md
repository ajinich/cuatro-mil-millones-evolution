# Human Evolution

## Origin and Timeline

Our species **Homo sapiens evolved in Africa**, with hominid species sharing a common ancestor with apes from approximately 7 million years ago in the Rift Valley region. Humans are **"the only living hominid species on Earth"** today.

## Evolutionary Lineage Framework

The text uses a generational framework to illustrate evolution:

- **Individual #100**: Someone from Egyptian times (identical to modern humans)
- **Individual #10,000**: Paleolithic human contemporary with Omo I, the oldest known *Homo sapiens* fossil
- **Individual #75,000**: *Homo erectus* (1.5 million years ago)
- **Individual #120,000**: Australopithecine resembling Lucy

## Key Adaptations Over Time

### Major Evolutionary Milestones
- **Bipedalism**: Emerged 3-4 million years ago as climatic changes converted forests to grasslands
- **Brain size**: Modern human brains average 1400 cc, developing 1-2 million years ago
- **Chin**: "We are the only hominid with this bony protuberance," appearing roughly 200,000 years ago
- **Opposable thumb**: Approximately 5 million years old, critical for tool manipulation

### Trait Ages
- Rhodopsin (eye molecule): 2+ billion years old
- Arm bones: 380 million years
- Hand structure: 375 million years (*Tiktaalik*)
- Middle ear bones: ~200 million years
- Fingernails: 55 million years

## Climate and Adaptation

Late Miocene cooling triggered forest expansion in East Africa, forcing some apes toward bipedal terrestrial movement—establishing australopithecines. Subsequent global cooling created African savannas, favoring fully bipedal hominids.

## Fossil Record Context

Darwin acknowledged the fossil record was **"imperfectly kept"**. The page notes that fossilization is rare because most dead organisms decompose before sediment covers them, yet existing fossils provide **"strong evidence for evolution"**.

## Methodological Insight

The continuous lineage concept: no individual in an ancestral chain differs dramatically from parent or child, yet distant ancestors become markedly different—comparable to daily photographs across a lifetime showing dramatic change without discrete transition moments.
